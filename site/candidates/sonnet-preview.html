A music player that remembers what you're tired of.

Harmonica is a local-first, self-hosted player for your own music library. Instead of ordinary shuffle, its recommendation algorithm spends your listening where it still has utility — so favourites don't get burned out and forgotten songs get another chance.

Runs on your own PC, a NAS, or a home server. No accounts, no cloud, nothing of yours leaves your network.

The problem with shuffle

Ordinary shuffle — and worse, an algorithm optimising for what you already like — runs your favourite songs into the ground. You end up hearing the same thirty tracks on a loop until you can't stand them, while hundreds of others in your library sit untouched. That's burnout, not variety, and it's the normal failure mode of "smart" playlists.

Harmonica treats listening as something with diminishing returns: a song, an artist, a whole topic can be temporarily used up. Its queue engine tracks cooldowns at the song level, the group level (artist or topic), and the sub-group level (a cover, remix, or alternate rendition of the same song), and applies a satiation guard that steps in before a favourite starts to sour. Songs you haven't heard or rated yet get deliberately boosted so the whole library gets sampled, not just the loudest favourites. The aim is a queue that still feels good the hundredth time you open it, not just the first.

What it actually does

Not shuffle

Weighted queue generation with per-song, per-group and per-sub-group cooldowns, so nothing gets repeated before it's had time to recover.

Satiation guard & rediscovery

Detects when you're bingeing a track or artist and eases off before it sours, and brings well-rated songs you haven't heard in a while back into rotation.

Cold-start coverage

Unheard and unrated tracks are boosted so new additions to your library actually get played, instead of sitting at the bottom of the pile forever.

Mood-normalised ratings

Star ratings adjust for how generous or harsh you're being in a given session, so a three-star rating means roughly the same thing every time you give one.

Cover & rendition families

A live take, a remix and the studio original can be grouped as one song for repetition purposes, instead of quietly doubling up as if they were unrelated tracks.

Real settings, and per-user profiles

Every control is an actual switch, slider or stepper with an explanation — never a config file to edit blind. On shared hardware, each person gets their own ratings, history and queue.

Setting it up

Harmonica is self-hosted: you run it yourself, on hardware you control. There's no managed hosting and no account on our servers — the only thing to sign up for is your own machine.

Once it's running, day-to-day use is simple. Getting it running the first time involves the usual self-hosting steps: cloning a repository, installing dependencies, pointing it at your media. Doing that by hand is tedious, so the intended path is to hand the repository to an AI coding agent (Claude Code, Cursor, or similar) and let it work through the setup. An agent-readable summary of this site is published at /llms.txt to make that easier.
